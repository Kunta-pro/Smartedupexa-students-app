Nutrients and Nutritional Disorders

3.1 Nutrition

Nutrition refers to the process by which living organisms obtain, consume, and use food substances to maintain their life processes (metabolic processes). These food substances are called nutrients.

Examples of nutrients:

  • In green plants: Water, Mineral salts, Carbon dioxide
  • In animals: Carbohydrates, Proteins, Lipids

3.2 Modes of Nutrition

Nutrition is broadly classified into two groups:

  • Heterotrophic nutrition: Nourishment on others
  • Autotrophic nutrition: Self-nourishment

Autotrophic Nutrition

This is a mode of nutrition where an organism synthesizes its own food from inorganic nutrients using external energy sources.

Types:

  • Photosynthesis (e.g., green plants, algae)
  • Chemosynthesis (e.g., chemosynthetic bacteria)
Image of Photosynthesis Process

Heterotrophic Nutrition

Organisms obtain food by feeding on already manufactured organic compounds. Examples include animals, fungi, and insectivorous plants.

Types of Heterotrophic Nutrition:

  • Parasitism
  • Saprophytic Nutrition
  • Symbiosis
  • Holozoic Nutrition

3.3 Food Nutrients

Food is any substance digested and absorbed by the body to maintain life processes. Food is required for growth, respiration, repair, and protection against diseases.

Classes of Food:

  • Energy giving foods (fats and oils)
  • Body-building foods (proteins)
  • Protective foods (vitamins, minerals)

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. They are classified as:

  • Monosaccharides
  • Disaccharides
  • Polysaccharides

Food Tests on Carbohydrates

Test Procedure Observation Deduction
Reducing Sugars Boil food solution with Benedict's solution. Blue solution persists, or green to brown precipitate forms. No reducing sugars or varying amounts of reducing sugars present.
Non-Reducing Sugars Hydrolyze with HCl, neutralize, then boil with Benedict's solution. Blue solution persists, or green to brown precipitate forms. No non-reducing sugars or varying amounts of non-reducing sugars present.
Starch Add iodine solution to food sample. Yellow to blue-black color change. No starch or varying amounts of starch present.

Nutrients and Nutritional Disorders

Proteins

Proteins are nutrients containing carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sometimes sulfur or phosphorus. The building blocks of proteins are amino acids.

Types of Amino Acids

  • Essential Amino Acids: Cannot be synthesized by the body; must be obtained from diet.
  • Non-Essential Amino Acids: Can be synthesized by the body.

Sources of Proteins

  • Eggs
  • Lean meat
  • Beans
  • Soya
  • Milk and its products
  • Fish
  • Groundnuts

Functions of Proteins

  • Body building and growth
  • Repair and regeneration of tissues
  • Synthesis of enzymes, hormones, and hemoglobin
  • Energy provision during starvation

Protein Deficiency

Deficiency in proteins leads to kwashiorkor. Symptoms include:

  • Loss of appetite
  • Diarrhea
  • Swollen lower abdomen (pot belly)
  • Wasted muscles

Biochemical Tests for Proteins

  • Biuret Test: Proteins form a violet complex with alkaline Copper (II) sulfate solution.
  • Million's Reagent: Forms a red coloration if proteins contain tyrosine.

Lipids (Fats and Oils)

Lipids are nutrients containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. They yield more energy than carbohydrates or proteins.

Properties of Lipids

  • Form translucent spots on paper
  • Insoluble in water

Functions of Lipids

  • Energy production
  • Body insulation
  • Shock absorption

3.5 Balanced Diet

A balanced diet is a meal containing all food nutrients in their right proportions. If a person depends on a poor diet (unbalanced diet), they may suffer from malnutrition.

Effects of a Balanced Diet

  • Helps in disease prevention
  • Facilitates weight loss
  • Promotes bone health
  • Improves mood
  • Enhances sleep patterns
  • Reduces cancer risks
  • Facilitates good growth
  • Improves memory
  • Enhances skin and hair growth

3.6 Malnutrition

This refers to an unhealthy state of the body resulting from a long-term deficiency or excess of one or more essential nutrients. Common deficiency diseases include kwashiorkor, marasmus, and obesity.

Eating Disorders

Conditions characterized by persistent disturbances in eating behavior, such as Anorexia Nervosa and Bulimia Nervosa.

Anorexia Nervosa

Characterized by abnormally low body weight.

  • Symptoms: Skipping meals, denial of hunger, spitting food out, weight loss.
  • Causes: Genetics, perfectionistic personality, past trauma, social attitudes.

Bulimia Nervosa

Characterized by episodes of uncontrolled overeating.

  • Symptoms: Eating excessive food, attempts to rid food.
  • Causes: Body dissatisfaction, stress, poor mental health, genetics.

3.8 Mineral Nutrition in Plants

Plants require mineral elements for growth, categorized into macro and micronutrients.

Essential Elements/Macronutrients

  • Nitrogen: Promotes leaf growth and protein formation. Deficiency leads to yellowing leaves and stunted growth.
  • Phosphorus: Encourages root and fruit development. Deficiency results in poor root growth and thin stalks.
  • Magnesium: Vital for chlorophyll formation. Deficiency causes yellow leaves and weak stems.
  • Potassium: Enhances disease resistance and crop quality. Deficiency leads to burnt leaf tips and poor seed quality.
  • Calcium: Strengthens cell walls and aids soil aeration. Deficiency causes rolled leaves and weak root tips.
  • Sulphur: Involved in protein synthesis. Deficiency results in chlorotic leaves and stunted growth.

Trace Elements/Micronutrients

  • Copper: Promotes Vitamin A formation and nitrogen regulation. Deficiency leads to chlorotic foliage.
  • Iron: Essential for chlorophyll and respiration. Deficiency causes yellowing leaf tips.
  • Manganese: Catalyzes growth processes. Deficiency leads to chlorosis in young leaves.
  • Molybdenum: Aids nitrogen utilization. Deficiency causes stunted growth.
  • Zinc: Associated with iron and manganese. Deficiency reduces fruiting.
