External Parts of a Typical Flowering Plant

A detailed look at the structure of a flowering plant.

7.1 External Parts of a Typical Flowering Plant

Flowering plants consist of two main systems:

  • Root system
  • Shoot system

These systems are made up of two categories of organs:

  • Reproductive organs: Involved in the reproduction of the plant, producing fruits and seeds.
  • Vegetative organs: Not directly involved in reproduction, including roots, stems, and leaves.

7.2 Parts of a Flowering Plant

LEAVES

A leaf is a thin, flattened structure growing from the nodes of a stem or branch. The leaf is made up of 3 main parts:

  • Leaf base: The part that attaches the leaf to the stem.
  • Petiole: The part connecting the leaf base to the leaf blade. Leaves with a petiole are called petiolate.
  • Lamina: The expanded and flattened portion of the leaf consisting of veins and midrib.

Venation

Venation refers to the arrangement of veins in the lamina of a leaf. There are two main types:

  • Network venation: Veins branch and intersect, forming a network. This is characteristic of dicots.
  • Parallel venation: Veins run side by side without branching, common in monocots.

Classification of Leaves

Leaves can be classified based on their structure:

  • Simple leaves: A single, undivided lamina (e.g., mango, hibiscus).
  • Compound leaves: Lamina completely divided into leaflets (e.g., beans, oxalis).

Types of Compound Leaves

Type of Leaf Description Example
Compound Pinnate Leaflets arranged along the midrib (rachis). Jacaranda
Compound Bipinnate Two orders of leaflets, further divided into pinnules. Jacaranda
Compound Digitate Leaflets radiate from the end of the petiole. Horse chestnut
Compound Trifoliate Three leaflets in the compound leaf. Soya bean

Leaf Arrangements on a Stem

Leaves are arranged on the stem in different ways:

  • Alternate: One leaf arises from each node.
  • Opposite: Two leaves arise from each node, opposite each other.
  • Whorled: More than two leaves arise from each node.

Functions of Leaves

The primary function of leaves is to manufacture food for the plant through photosynthesis. Other functions include:

  • Gas exchange (oxygen and carbon dioxide) via stomata.
  • Transpiration to remove excess water.

Modified Leaves

Some plants have modified leaves for specific purposes:

  • Leaf Tendrils: Used by climbing plants for support.
  • Leaf Spines: Sharp structures for defense.
  • Scale Leaves: Protect axillary buds from damage.
  • Insectivorous Leaves: Capture and digest insects for nutrients.

Summary

This concludes the first part of the topic on the external parts of a flowering plant.

7.1 Roots

A root is a descending portion of the axis of the plant and develops from the radical of the embryo during germination.

Types of Roots

1. Tap Root System

  • Consists of a main root growing straight downwards.
  • It gives rise to side roots called lateral roots.
  • Tap root system is characteristic of dicotyledonous plants.

2. Fibrous Root System

  • No main root; all roots arise from the base of the stem.
  • Roots are almost of the same size.
  • Characteristic of monocotyledonous plants.

Functions of Roots

  • Anchor the plant firmly in the soil.
  • Absorb water and mineral salts from the soil.
  • Conduct absorbed water and minerals to the leaves.
  • Store food in some plants (e.g., cassava).
  • Some roots are modified for breathing (e.g., white mangrove).

Modified Roots

1. Storage Roots

  • Thick, fleshy, and succulent roots.
  • Contain stored food like sugar and starch (e.g., carrots, cassava, sweet potatoes).

2. Breathing Roots

  • Found in swampy areas (e.g., white mangrove).
  • Roots grow up through mud to the air; parts above the mud are spongy and absorb air.

3. Stilt Roots

  • Develop from the main stem, found in plants like red mangrove.
  • Provide additional support to plants in muddy areas.

4. Prop Roots

  • Grow from nodes of plants like maize, sorghum, and sugar cane.
  • Provide extra support by holding the plant firmly to the soil surface.

5. Buttress Roots

  • Large, thick roots growing from the base of plants like Mvule trees, silk cotton.
  • Provide extra support to anchor the plant firmly in the soil.

6. Clasping Roots

  • Found in climbing stems like figs, vanilla, and orchids.
  • Secrete a sticky substance to cling to other plants for support.

7. Epiphytic Roots

  • Found on epiphytes, plants that grow on other plants.
  • Roots hang freely in the air and absorb moisture from the atmosphere.

7.2 Stems

The stem is the ascending portion of the plant axis that develops from the plumule of the embryo.

Functions of Stems

  • Hold leaves in the best position for sunlight to perform photosynthesis.
  • Conduct water and minerals from roots to leaves and food from leaves to other parts of the plant.
  • Support flowers and fruits for pollination or dispersal.
  • Young stems perform photosynthesis and produce food for the plant.
  • Stems have lenticels for gaseous exchange.

Types of Stems

Erect Stems

  • Support themselves in an upright position; can be woody or herbaceous.
  • Woody stems are high in lignin, found in shrubs and trees.
  • Herbaceous stems contain little or no woody material (e.g., tomato, rice).

Weak Stems

  • Cannot support themselves upright and either creep or climb for support.

Underground Stems

  • Remain permanently underground and often serve as food storage organs.

Modified Stems

1. Twinning Stems (Twiners)

  • Stems that grow ascending spirally around a support (e.g., Dutch man’s pipe, lianas).

2. Climbing Stems

  • Cling to supports through tendrils, thin wire-like coiled branches (e.g., passion fruit).

3. Creeping Stems (Creepers)

  • Long, thin stems that grow along the ground and give off roots at the nodes (e.g., oxalis).
  • Subtypes include runners, offsets, suckers.

Underground Stems (continued)

1. Rhizomes

  • Horizontal, thick underground stems with adventitious roots (e.g., ginger, couch grass).

2. Stem Tubers

  • Short, fleshy underground stems swollen with stored food (e.g., Irish potato, yams).

3. Stolons

  • Horizonally growing stems that root at nodes and produce new plants (e.g., strawberry).

4. Bulbs

  • Short, conical-shaped underground stems with concentric layers of fleshy leaves (e.g., onion, garlic).

5. Corms

  • Swollen, fleshy underground stems with a terminal bud (e.g., cocoyam, crocus).
