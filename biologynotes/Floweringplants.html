External Parts of a Typical Flowering Plant

A detailed look at the structure of a flowering plant.

7.1 External Parts of a Typical Flowering Plant

Flowering plants consist of two main systems:

  • Root system
  • Shoot system

These systems are made up of two categories of organs:

  • Reproductive organs: Involved in the reproduction of the plant, producing fruits and seeds.
  • Vegetative organs: Not directly involved in reproduction, including roots, stems, and leaves.

7.2 Parts of a Flowering Plant

LEAVES

A leaf is a thin, flattened structure growing from the nodes of a stem or branch. The leaf is made up of 3 main parts:

  • Leaf base: The part that attaches the leaf to the stem.
  • Petiole: The part connecting the leaf base to the leaf blade. Leaves with a petiole are called petiolate.
  • Lamina: The expanded and flattened portion of the leaf consisting of veins and midrib.

Venation

Venation refers to the arrangement of veins in the lamina of a leaf. There are two main types:

  • Network venation: Veins branch and intersect, forming a network. This is characteristic of dicots.
  • Parallel venation: Veins run side by side without branching, common in monocots.

Classification of Leaves

Leaves can be classified based on their structure:

  • Simple leaves: A single, undivided lamina (e.g., mango, hibiscus).
  • Compound leaves: Lamina completely divided into leaflets (e.g., beans, oxalis).

Types of Compound Leaves

Type of Leaf Description Example
Compound Pinnate Leaflets arranged along the midrib (rachis). Jacaranda
Compound Bipinnate Two orders of leaflets, further divided into pinnules. Jacaranda
Compound Digitate Leaflets radiate from the end of the petiole. Horse chestnut
Compound Trifoliate Three leaflets in the compound leaf. Soya bean

Leaf Arrangements on a Stem

Leaves are arranged on the stem in different ways:

  • Alternate: One leaf arises from each node.
  • Opposite: Two leaves arise from each node, opposite each other.
  • Whorled: More than two leaves arise from each node.

Functions of Leaves

The primary function of leaves is to manufacture food for the plant through photosynthesis. Other functions include:

  • Gas exchange (oxygen and carbon dioxide) via stomata.
  • Transpiration to remove excess water.

Modified Leaves

Some plants have modified leaves for specific purposes:

  • Leaf Tendrils: Used by climbing plants for support.
  • Leaf Spines: Sharp structures for defense.
  • Scale Leaves: Protect axillary buds from damage.
  • Insectivorous Leaves: Capture and digest insects for nutrients.

Summary
