Classification

Definitions

Taxonomy: The branch of biology dealing with naming and classification of living organisms.

Classification: The act of putting living things into groups based on similar characteristics. A group of organisms with similar characteristics is called a taxon (plural: taxa).

Carolus Linnaeus

In 1753, Carolus Linnaeus introduced a modern method of classifying organisms.

Types of Classification

  • Natural Classification: Based on natural order or common ancestry informed by science (e.g., the cat family).
  • Artificial Classification: Based on superficial characteristics, imaginations, or human uses (e.g., parasites or pests).

Comparison Between Natural and Artificial Classification

Natural Classification Artificial Classification
Many characteristics are considered. Only a few characteristics are considered.
Members are similar in hereditary patterns. Members are not similar in hereditary patterns.
Stable and modern method of classification. May change as knowledge about organisms changes.
Provides plenty of useful information. Provides only limited information.

Importance of Classification

  • Makes it easier to study organisms by grouping them based on characteristics.
  • Helps understand evolutionary relationships (phylogeny).
  • Prevents confusion by organizing information.
  • Provides a universal method for arranging information.

Levels of Classification

The seven levels of taxonomy, from smallest to largest:

  1. Species: A group of organisms capable of producing fertile offspring.
  2. Genus: A group of closely related species.
  3. Family: A group of closely related genera.
  4. Order: A group of closely related families.
  5. Class: A group of closely related orders.
  6. Phylum: A group of closely related classes.
  7. Kingdom: The largest taxonomic group containing related phyla.

Scientific Naming of Living Organisms

The binomial system gives organisms two Latin names: the genus name and the species name.

Dichotomous Key

A dichotomous key is a tool that uses two contrasting statements to identify organisms.

Example:

  • 1. Compound or simple leaf
    • (a) Compound leaf ... go to 2
    • (b) Simple leaf ... go to 3
  • 2. Arrangement of leaflets
    • (a) Digitate arrangement ... Cassava
    • (b) Pinnate arrangement ... Eucalyptus
