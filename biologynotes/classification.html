Classification

Definitions

The branch of biology dealing with naming and classification of living organisms is called taxonomy.

Classification is the act of putting living things together into groups on the basis of the characteristics they have in common. A group of organisms with similar characteristics is called a taxon (plural: taxa).

Carolus Linnaeus

A scientist named Carolus Linnaeus introduced a modern way of classifying organisms in 1753.

Types of Classification

  • Natural Classification: Based on natural order or common ancestry informed by science (e.g., the cat family).
  • Artificial Classification: Based on superficial characteristics, imaginations, or human uses (e.g., parasites or pests).

Comparison Between Natural and Artificial Classification

Natural Classification Artificial Classification
Many characteristics are considered. Only a few characteristics are considered.
Members of a group are similar in hereditary patterns. Members are not similar in hereditary patterns.
Stable and most modern method of classification. May change as knowledge about organisms changes.
Provides plenty of useful information. Provides only limited information.

Importance of Classification

  • Makes it easy to study organisms by grouping them based on similar characteristics.
  • Helps understand the evolutionary relationship (phylogeny) between different organisms.
  • Prevents confusion around the scientific world by organizing information about organisms.
  • Provides a universal way of arranging information about organisms.

Levels of Classification

There are seven levels of taxonomy:

  1. Species: A group of organisms with similar characteristics capable of breeding freely to produce fertile offspring.
  2. Genus: A group of closely related species.
  3. Family: A group of closely related genera.
  4. Order: A group of closely related families.
  5. Class: A group of closely related orders.
  6. Phylum: A group of closely related classes.
  7. Kingdom: A group of organisms belonging to closely related phyla.

Scientific Naming of Living Organisms

The binomial system gives two unique Latin names to an organism, beginning with the genus name followed by the species name.

Dichotomous Key

The word "dichotomy" means divided into two parts. A dichotomous key is a set of instructions used to identify unknown organisms. It provides two contrasting statements leading to a group or specific organism.

Example:

  1. Compound or simple leaf
    • (a) Compound leaf (leaf divided into leaflets) ... go to 2
    • (b) Simple leaf (leaf not divided into leaflets) ... go to 3
  2. Arrangement of leaflets
    • (a) Digitate arrangement of leaflets ... Cassava
    • (b) Pinnate arrangement of leaflets ... Eucalyptus
