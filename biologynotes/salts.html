Natural sources of salt are sea water and rocks which consist of:

Sodium chloride (common salt) NaCl
Sodium sulphate Na₂SO₄
Calcium sulphate CaSO₄
Calcium carbonate CaCO₃

Types of Salts

Types of Salts

Introduction

There are four types of salts:

  • Normal salt
  • Acidic salt
  • Basic salt
  • Double salt

Normal Salt

Normal salt is the salt formed when all the replaceable hydrogen ions of an acid are replaced by a metal.

Examples:

  • Potassium carbonate – K₂CO₃
  • Sodium sulphate – Na₂SO₄
  • Ammonium sulphate – (NH₄)₂SO₄
  • Zinc chloride – ZnCl₂

Acidic Salt

Acidic salt is the salt formed when only part of the replaceable hydrogen ions of an acid are replaced by a metal.

Formation Examples:

Stage Reaction
First Stage NaOH (aq) + CO₂ (g) → Na₂CO₃ (aq)
Second Stage Na₂CO₃ (aq) + H₂O (l) + CO₂ (g) → NaHCO₃ (aq)

Other Acidic Salts:

  • Sodium hydrogen sulphate – NaHSO₄
  • Potassium hydrogen carbonate – KHCO₃
  • Sodium hydrogen phosphate – NaHPO₄

Note: Monobasic acids cannot form acidic salts.

Basic Salt

Basic salt is formed when the amount of acid required to neutralize a base is insufficient.

Examples:

  • Basic zinc chloride – [ZnCl₂ · Zn(OH)₂]
  • Basic magnesium chloride – [MgCl₂ · Mg(OH)₂]
  • Basic lead carbonate – [PbCO₃ · Pb(OH)₂]

Double Salt

Double salt is formed when two solutions of different salts are mixed together and allowed to stand, they react to form a new single salt different from the original ones.

Example:

(NH₄)₂SO₄ (aq) + Fe₂(SO₄)₃ (aq) → [FeNH₄(SO₄)] (aq)

Preparation of Soluble Salts

Preparation of Soluble Salts

1. Reaction Between Acid and Metal

Equation:

  • Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas
  • HCl(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
  • HNO₃(aq) + Zn(s) → Zn(NO₃)₂(aq) + H₂(g)
NB: This reaction is not suitable for very reactive metals like potassium, sodium, or calcium as the reaction would be too fast and can cause an explosion.

2. Reaction Between Acid and Alkalis (Neutralization)

Equation:

  • Acid + Alkali → Salt + Water
  • HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) + CaO(aq) → CaSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l)
NB: Water is removed by evaporation to get crystals.

3. Reaction Between Acid and Insoluble Base

Equation:

  • Acid + Insoluble base → Salt + Water
  • 2HCl(aq) + CuO(s) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) + ZnO(s) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l)
NB: Water is removed by evaporation to get crystals.

4. Reaction Between Acids and Metal Carbonates

Equation:

  • Acid + Metal carbonate → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
  • 2HCl(aq) + CaCO₃(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) + CaCO₃(aq) → CaSO₄(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
  • 2HNO₃(aq) + CaCO₃(aq) → Ca(NO₃)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
NB: Water is removed by evaporation to get crystals.

5. Reaction Between Acids and Metal Hydrogen Carbonates

Equation:

  • Acid + Hydrogen carbonate → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
  • HCl(aq) + Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) + Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq) → CaSO₄(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
  • HNO₃(aq) + Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq) → Ca(NO₃)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
NB: Water is removed by evaporation to get crystals.

6. Direct Combination

Equation:

  • Salt (anhydrous) can be formed by direct combination (synthesis):
  • 2Fe(s) + 3Cl₂(g) → 2FeCl₃(s)
