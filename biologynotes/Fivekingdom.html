Five Kingdoms of Living Organisms

4.1 Kingdoms of Living Organisms

There are five kingdoms of living organisms today:

  • Monera
  • Protoctista
  • Fungi
  • Plantae
  • Animalia

Note:

Viruses are not classified in any of the five kingdoms because they do not have all the characteristics of living things. For example:

  • They lack cellular structures like cytoplasm and organelles.
  • They use the nuclear material and organelles of other living organisms to carry out their metabolic processes.
  • They can survive outside their host’s cell as inert organic crystals.

4.2 Kingdom Monera

This kingdom includes the simplest and smallest living organisms known as bacteria. These organisms are single-celled.

Structure of a Bacteria

Characteristics of Bacteria

  • They are unicellular (single-celled) organisms.
  • They have no true nucleus; their genetic material is not enclosed by a nuclear membrane.
  • Bacteria exist in various shapes:
    • Rounded (Cocci)
    • Coiled (Spirilla)
    • Rod-shaped (Bacilli)
  • They exist singly or in colonies:
    • In pairs, e.g., Diplococci and Diplobacilli
    • In clusters, e.g., Staphylococci
    • In chain forms, e.g., Streptococci

Economic Importance of Bacteria

Harmful Effects:

  • They cause diseases, e.g., tuberculosis and cholera in humans.
  • Bacteria cause food to rot.
  • Some bacteria reduce soil fertility by denitrification.

Useful Effects:

  • They help in the production of compost manure and biogas.
  • Some bacteria improve soil fertility by fixing nitrogen.
  • They are used in the treatment of sewage.
  • Some bacteria are used in industries, e.g., making yogurt and medicine.
  • Bacteria in ruminants secrete cellulase, which aids in the digestion of cellulose in grass.

Adaptations of Bacteria

  • They can live in adverse conditions.
  • They respire both aerobically and anaerobically.
  • Bacteria have various modes of feeding, reducing competition.
  • They mutate very fast, producing many strains.
  • Bacteria are easily dispersed because of their small size.
  • They have a high reproductive rate, increasing their survival chances.
  • They thrive in various environments like water, air, and soil.

4.3 Kingdom Protoctista

This kingdom consists of single-celled and simple multicellular organisms that possess a true nucleus, unlike Monera.

Main Features of Protoctista

  • They are unicellular organisms.
  • They have a true nucleus with a nuclear membrane.
  • They possess double-membrane organelles.
  • Some members locomote freely using:
    • Pseudopodia (false legs) in amoeba
    • Cilia in paramecium
    • Flagella in euglena and trypanosomes
  • They exhibit varied forms of nutrition, e.g.:
    • Photosynthesis in euglena and Chlamydomonas
    • Phagocytosis in amoeba and paramecium
    • Absorption of digested food in trypanosomes
  • They live mostly in water or watery environments like wetlands.

4.4 Kingdom Fungi

Kingdom fungi includes organisms like mushrooms, yeast, and moulds. They grow in wood, soil, and develop from tiny spores.

Characteristics of Fungi

  • Fungi have a true nucleus, and their cells have a cell wall made of chitin.
  • They do not make their own food but feed on decomposing organic matter of animals and plants.
  • Fungi store excess carbohydrates in the form of glycogen, similar to animals.

Economic Importance of Fungi

Useful Aspects:

  • They recycle organic material, keeping soil fertile.
  • Some fungi, like mushrooms, are edible.
  • Used in medicine production, e.g., the antibiotic penicillin.
  • Yeast is used in baking and brewing industries.

Harmful Aspects:

  • Cause diseases such as ringworm, candida, athlete’s foot, potato blight, and leaf rust.
  • Fungi spoil food through decomposition.

Structure of Bread Mould

Bread mould is the most common decomposer in stored foods. It thrives in humid and damp conditions.

Effects of Bread Mould:

  • Introduces mycotoxins which may cause digestive system issues.
  • Signs and symptoms include:
    • Vomiting
    • Stomach pain
    • Diarrhea
    • Fever

Structure of Mushroom

The body of a mushroom consists of a network of multinucleated threads called hyphae.

  • Sporangiophore: Vertical hyphae bearing a spore case (sporangium) at the tip.
  • Stolon: Horizontal hyphae.
  • Rhizoids: Root-like hyphae for nutrient absorption.

The total mass of hyphae is called the mycelium.

4.5 Kingdom Plantae

Kingdom Plantae consists of plants in various forms and sizes, including mosses, ferns, grasses, shrubs, and trees.

Characteristics of Plants

  • Contain the green pigment chlorophyll for photosynthesis.
  • Cells are surrounded by a cellulose cell wall.
  • Multicellular organisms with a nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane.

Classification of Plants

Plants are grouped into four divisions based on structure and reproduction:

Division Bryophyta

Examples: Mosses, liverworts, and hornworts.

  • Small, non-vascular, non-flowering plants.
  • Bear stems, small leaf-like structures, and rhizoids.
  • Reproduce by producing spores.

Division Pteridophyta

Examples: Ferns.

  • Vascular, non-flowering plants.
  • Have leaves, stems, and roots.
  • Reproduce by spores, clustered in sporangia called sori.

Division Gymnospermae

These are vascular, seed-producing plants, e.g., conifers.

Division Angiospermae

Flowering plants that produce seeds enclosed in fruit.

Kingdom Animalia

Kingdom Animalia

Characteristics of Animals

  • Animals have locomotory devices like wings, legs, or fins.
  • They possess sense organs and a well-developed nervous system.
  • Animals are heterotrophic, feeding on other organisms.
  • Multicellular organisms with membrane-bound nuclei.
  • They lack cell walls, unlike plants and fungi.

Classification of Animals

The animal kingdom is divided into 9 main groups (phyla):

  • Eight are invertebrates (no backbone).
  • One group includes vertebrates (animals with a backbone).

Invertebrates

Phylum: Arthropoda

The largest animal phylum, arthropods, are characterized by:

  • A segmented body (head, thorax, abdomen).
  • Jointed appendages for movement.
  • A hard exoskeleton made of chitin, preventing water loss.
Classes of Arthropods:
1. Insects
  • Three main body divisions: head, thorax, and abdomen.
  • Three pairs of legs.
  • Examples: Butterfly, housefly, cockroach.
2. Arachnids
  • Two body parts: cephalothorax and abdomen.
  • Four pairs of legs, no antennae or wings.
  • Examples: Spider, tick, scorpion.
3. Crustaceans
  • Two body parts: cephalothorax and abdomen.
  • Five pairs of legs; some with pincers.
  • Examples: Crab, prawn, crayfish.
4. Myriapods
  • Elongated, segmented bodies.
  • Examples: Centipede, millipede.

Phylum: Chordata

Chordates are distinguished by:

  • A notochord present in early development.
  • A dorsal nerve cord and a post-anal tail at some stage.

Classes of Vertebrates

1. Class: Pisces (Fishes)

Two subclasses:

  • Chondrichthyes: Cartilaginous fish like sharks.
  • Osteichthyes: Bony fish like tilapia.
2. Class: Amphibia (Amphibians)
  • Amphibians live on land and water.
  • Examples: Frogs, toads, salamanders.
3. Class: Reptilia (Reptiles)
  • Examples: Snake, lizard, crocodile.
  • Characteristics: Internal fertilization, dry scales.
4. Class: Aves (Birds)
  • Examples: Eagle, duck, flamingo.
  • Characteristics: Feathers for flight and warmth, hollow bones.
5. Class: Mammalia (Mammals)
  • Examples: Lion, whale, humans.
  • Characteristics: Fur or hair, mammary glands, live birth.
