Soil and Its Importance

Soil is a finely divided material covering the Earth's crust or surface. It consists of:

  • Air
  • Water
  • Humus
  • Living organisms
  • Weathered rocks

Importance of Soil

  • Soil provides nutrients like water and minerals to plants, which are the chief producers of food in the environment.
  • It is a habitat for many organisms such as earthworms, termites, bacteria, fungi, and arthropods.
  • Soil acts as a medium for disposing of wastes for humans and other animals.
  • It is an important natural resource for construction materials, agriculture, crafts, and art materials.

Soil Formation

Soil is formed from parent rocks through the process of weathering over several years. Weathering occurs in three main ways:

1. Physical Weathering

  • Alternate heating and cooling: Causes expansion and contraction of rocks, leading to cracking and breaking.
  • Water: Rivers and streams wear away rocks.
  • Sandstorms: Wind blows sand against bare rocks.
  • Frosting: Freezing water in cracks expands, causing rocks to break.

2. Chemical Weathering

  • Rain dissolves carbon dioxide to form carbonic acid, which dissolves soft rocks like limestone.
  • Hot, damp conditions lead to oxidation of rocks, forming soil.

3. Biological Weathering

  • Organisms like lichens and small plants grow on rocks, contributing to humus formation.
  • Humans split rocks directly during construction and indirectly through cultivation.

Factors Influencing Soil Formation

Five major factors influence soil formation:

Factor Explanation
Climate Rainfall and temperature promote chemical and physical breakdown of parent rocks.
Living Organisms Vegetation cover and microorganisms decompose organic material, enriching soil.
Parent Rock Determines soil's physical properties and mineral content.
Topography Steep slopes lead to erosion, affecting soil depth and composition.
Time Longer formation time results in deeper, mature soils.

Types of Soil

Soil is classified based on particle size and nature into three main types:

Sandy Soil

  • Large spaces between particles allow water to drain quickly.
  • Gritty feel when wet and low in calcium and magnesium.
  • Described as light soils, easy to work with.

Clay Soil

  • Fine particles with poor drainage, prone to waterlogging.
  • Sticky feel when wet and difficult to work with, called heavy soils.

Loam Soil

A mixture of sand, silt, clay, and organic matter, with a stable crumb structure, making it ideal for crop production.
