Soil and Its Importance

Soil is a finely divided material covering the Earth's crust or surface. It consists of:

  • Air
  • Water
  • Humus
  • Living organisms
  • Weathered rocks

Importance of Soil

  • Soil provides nutrients like water and minerals to plants, which are the chief producers of food in the environment.
  • It is a habitat for many organisms such as earthworms, termites, bacteria, fungi, and arthropods.
  • Soil acts as a medium for disposing of wastes for humans and other animals.
  • It is an important natural resource for construction materials, agriculture, crafts, and art materials.

Soil Formation

Soil is formed from parent rocks through the process of weathering over several years. Weathering occurs in three main ways:

1. Physical Weathering

  • Alternate heating and cooling: Causes expansion and contraction of rocks, leading to cracking and breaking.
  • Water: Rivers and streams wear away rocks.
  • Sandstorms: Wind blows sand against bare rocks.
  • Frosting: Freezing water in cracks expands, causing rocks to break.

2. Chemical Weathering

  • Rain dissolves carbon dioxide to form carbonic acid, which dissolves soft rocks like limestone.
  • Hot, damp conditions lead to oxidation of rocks, forming soil.

3. Biological Weathering

  • Organisms like lichens and small plants grow on rocks, contributing to humus formation.
  • Humans split rocks directly during construction and indirectly through cultivation.

Factors Influencing Soil Formation

Five major factors influence soil formation:

Factor Explanation
Climate Rainfall and temperature promote chemical and physical breakdown of parent rocks.
Living Organisms Vegetation cover and microorganisms decompose organic material, enriching soil.
Parent Rock Determines soil's physical properties and mineral content.
Topography Steep slopes lead to erosion, affecting soil depth and composition.
Time Longer formation time results in deeper, mature soils.

Types of Soil

Soil is classified based on particle size and nature into three main types:

Sandy Soil

  • Large spaces between particles allow water to drain quickly.
  • Gritty feel when wet and low in calcium and magnesium.
  • Described as light soils, easy to work with.

Clay Soil

  • Fine particles with poor drainage, prone to waterlogging.
  • Sticky feel when wet and difficult to work with, called heavy soils.

Loam Soil

A mixture of sand, silt, clay, and organic matter, with a stable crumb structure, making it ideal for crop production.

Soil Profile and Components

1.2 Soil Layers

Soil consists of distinct layers, referred to as horizons, that form the vertical arrangement of the soil profile. A soil profile with clearly defined layers is mature, while one without is considered young or immature. The main layers include:

  • Topsoil
  • Subsoil
  • Parent Rock

Diagram to show the soil profile:

Horizon A – Topsoil

This is the uppermost layer, approximately 20cm deep. It is the most important horizon for supporting plant growth.

  • Better aerated than other layers.
  • Rich in active soil microorganisms and humus, giving it a dark color.
  • Contains more plant roots and litter.

Horizon B – Subsoil

This is a thicker, light-brown layer beneath the topsoil, mainly composed of rock fragments, clay, and gravel.

  • Less aerated compared to topsoil.
  • Contains few deep plant roots and minimal organisms.
  • Very little or no humus present.
  • Rich in mineral salts due to leaching, referred to as the layer of accumulation.

Horizon C – Parent Rock

This is the solid rock layer found below the subsoil, representing the original unweathered parent material.

  • Composed of granite, volcanic rocks, and sedimentary rocks.
  • Lacks humus, has low air content, and contains minimal mineral salts.

1.3 Components of Soil

Soil is composed of six main components:

  • Inorganic particles
  • Humus
  • Water
  • Air
  • Mineral salts
  • Soil living organisms

Inorganic Particles

These are formed through the weathering process and vary in size. They include clay, silt, fine sand, coarse sand, and gravel.

Soil Particle Diameter (mm)
Gravel >2.0
Coarse Sand 0.2–2.0
Fine Sand 0.02–0.2
Silt 0.002–0.02
Clay <0.002

Uses:

  • Provides anchorage for plant roots, supporting plants.
  • Gives a rigid framework to soil.
  • Supplies mineral elements to plants.

Soil Air

Soil air exists in the spaces between particles and is vital for plant roots and soil organisms. It consists mainly of oxygen and nitrogen.

Importance:

  • Provides oxygen for respiration of organisms and roots.
  • Facilitates decay processes that produce humus.
  • Supplies nitrogen for fixation by bacteria, aiding in nitrate formation.
  • Helps in weathering by forming carbonic acid with CO2.

Soil Water

Soil water comes from rain and groundwater, forming a thin film around particles.

Functions:

  • Maintains soil moisture for microorganism survival.
  • Dissolves mineral salts for plant absorption.
  • Supports plant cell rigidity, aiding herbaceous plant structure.
  • Raw material for photosynthesis.

Humus

Humus is the decayed remains of plants and animals, including fallen leaves, dead bodies, and droppings. It is dark brown, sticky, and crucial for soil health.

Importance:

  • Enhances soil warmth, aiding seed germination.
  • Improves water retention and aeration.
  • Provides nutrients and stabilizes soil pH.
  • Encourages soil organism activity by providing food and shelter.
  • Improves soil structure by binding particles into clumps.
