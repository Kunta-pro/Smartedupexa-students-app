5.1 Enzymes

Enzymes are organic compounds, protein in nature, that speed up biochemical reactions in the body without being altered at the end of the reaction.

Naming of Enzymes

Enzymes are named by adding the suffix "-ase" to their substrates.

Name of Substrate Name of Enzyme
LipidsLipase
SucroseSucrase
MaltoseMaltase
PeptidesPeptidase
ProteinsProtease
CelluloseCellulase
ATPATPase

Structure of an Enzyme

An enzyme consists of amino acids folded into a 3D shape, forming an active site, where the substrate binds to form an enzyme-substrate complex, initiating catalysis.

Importance of Enzymes

  • Speed up biochemical reactions.
  • Control metabolic processes to maintain normal body functions.

5.2 Properties and Factors Affecting Enzymes

  • Protein in nature and specific in action.
  • Effective in small amounts and remain unchanged after reactions.
  • Denatured by high temperatures and inactivated by extreme pH levels.

5.3 Mammalian Teeth

Each tooth consists of three parts: crown, neck, and root. The internal structures include enamel, dentine, pulp cavity, gum, and cement.

5.4 Dental Formula

The dental formula represents the number of each type of tooth in half the upper and lower jaws.

Adult Human Dental Formula: I: 2/2, C: 1/1, PM: 2/2, M: 3/3 = 32

5.5 Oral Hygiene

Proper dental care includes regular brushing, avoiding sugary foods, and eating calcium-rich foods. Common dental issues include tooth decay and periodontal diseases.

Digestion in Man

Digestion is the process by which complex food substances are broken down into simpler, soluble compounds that the body can absorb and utilize. It involves two main processes:

  • Physical Digestion: Mechanical breakdown of food by teeth, muscular contractions, and bile juice.
  • Chemical Digestion: Breakdown of food through enzymatic action.

Steps in Digestion

  • Ingestion: Taking in food into the body.
  • Digestion: Breaking down food into absorbable forms.
  • Egestion: Expulsion of undigested food as feces.

The Human Alimentary Canal

The alimentary canal consists of the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and associated organs like the liver and pancreas.

Digestion in the Mouth

Physical Digestion

  • Chewing increases food surface area for enzyme action.
  • Food is mixed with saliva, softening it and forming a bolus for easier swallowing.

Chemical Digestion

  • Salivary amylase breaks down starch into maltose.
  • Saliva is alkaline, providing an optimal pH for enzyme activity.

The Act of Swallowing

  • The epiglottis prevents food from entering the trachea.
  • The soft palate closes the nasal passage to prevent food entry.
  • Peristalsis moves food through the esophagus.

Diagram: Peristalsis in the Esophagus

Digestion in the Stomach

  • Gastric juice contains pepsin, HCl, and mucus.
  • Pepsin breaks down proteins into polypeptides.
  • HCl kills bacteria, activates pepsin, and stops salivary amylase.
  • Mucus protects the stomach lining from self-digestion.

Digestion in the Duodenum

  • Chyme from the stomach enters the duodenum in small amounts.
  • Bile emulsifies fats, while pancreatic juice contains enzymes for protein, carbohydrate, and fat digestion.

Digestion in the Ileum

  • Final digestion occurs in the ileum.
  • Soluble end products like glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids are absorbed.

Absorption and Assimilation

Absorption

The process by which nutrients pass through the villi in the small intestine into the bloodstream or lymphatic system.

  • The ileum is adapted with villi and microvilli to increase the surface area for absorption.
  • Glucose and amino acids enter blood capillaries, while fatty acids and glycerol enter lacteals.

Diagram: Structure of a Villus

Assimilation

Assimilation is the incorporation of absorbed nutrients into body metabolism for growth, repair, and energy production.

The Liver

  • Produces bile for lipid digestion.
  • Stores glycogen, iron, and vitamins.
  • Detoxifies harmful substances like ammonia.
  • Produces plasma proteins for blood clotting.
